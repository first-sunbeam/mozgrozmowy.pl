<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Brain, what exactly is all this ABA about? Many people argue, some say it's helpful, others – that it's training…" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Alright, here's the quick update. ABA — Applied Behavior Analysis, in Polish *Stosowana Analiza Zachowania*. It is an evidence-based approach built on the idea that behavior has a function, and that if we understand why something happens, we can teach better strategies." />

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So it's not about punishing or forcing?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="In theory – no. It's about supporting what works." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="For example: if a child does not speak, ABA may help them learn communication — verbal, pictorial, gestural, any kind. But... exactly: everything depends on HOW it is done." />

## History of ABA: from training to support

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Exactly! Because I heard that once ABA was some horror…" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Oh yes. In the 1960s–80s, one version of ABA was especially popular — the kind where the goal was to “fix” the child so they would look “normal”." />

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So... pure training?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="To a large extent yes. And many people who went through this still talk about trauma today. But fortunately – it's changing. " />

<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>In that version of ABA, things often looked like this:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>stimming or other forms of self-regulation — forbidden,</li>
		<li>lack of eye contact — punished,</li>
		<li>anger — ignored.</li>
	</ul>
	<p>What mattered was only this: “Do what you are told.”</p>
</DialogLine>

## Modern ABA: a different approach

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Okay, so what is this 'modern ABA'?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>Today, ABA more often moves toward:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>supporting communication rather than suppressing it,</li>
		<li>learning through relationship rather than coercion,</li>
		<li>taking sensory experience and emotions into account,</li>
		<li>respecting refusal — because “I don’t want to” is an important skill.</li>
	</ul>
</DialogLine>

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And do people really teach it like this now?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="In good centers — yes. There are also organizations that monitor standards, such as BACB (Behavior Analyst Certification Board) or CASP (Council of Autism Service Providers)." />

<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="But watch out — not all ABA looks the same, and that is the biggest problem." />

## Kupferstein Study (2018): PTSD and ABA?

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And that whole article saying ABA causes PTSD?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Kupferstein (2018) – I know. This study caused a storm because it says autistic people after ABA have PTSD symptoms." />

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So this is proof that ABA = trauma?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="But... the study had important limitations: it relied on an online survey, without PTSD diagnosis and without a control group. On that basis, you cannot simply say that ABA = trauma." />

<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="But — and this is the important part — if therapy is conducted rigidly, without consent and without regard for emotions, it can be deeply burdensome." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So what matters is the way it is used, not just the label?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Exactly. ABA is a tool. You can use it to support development, but you can also overwhelm and suppress if it is used without reflection." />

## Parents and therapy choice

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And what about those parents who use ABA?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Let's not judge them too quickly. Many of them simply want to help their child — but sometimes no one has told them that there are different versions of ABA, and that they have the right to ask questions, check things carefully, and raise concerns. " />

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Okay. But if I wanted to make sure the ABA I find is actually okay?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>Then ask better questions than just: “is this ABA?”</p>
	<p>Ask:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>can my child say “no”,</li>
		<li>does anyone take their sensory needs and emotions into account,</li>
		<li>is anyone teaching them self-awareness, rather than only “good behavior”.</li>
	</ul>
</DialogLine>

## Alternative approaches: PBS, ACT, trauma-informed

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And PBS, ACT, trauma-informed? Are they better?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>They also draw on behavior analysis, but they focus on relationship, values, and wellbeing — not only on outcomes.</p>
	<p>Even analysts themselves increasingly say this out loud — for example, McGill and Robinson (2020) wrote an important article about moving away from behavior management and toward supporting the person.</p>
</DialogLine>

## ABA in Poland: a free-for-all?

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Okay, all of this sounds like a description of the US... So what does the situation look like in Poland?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="(with a sigh) Ahh, Poland... exactly. ABA exists here too, but it is a completely different story." />

<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="We do not have a system like in the US — no national supervision, no mandatory ethical code." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="There are therapists with the international BCBA certification, but there are only a handful of them." />

### So everything depends on who you happen to find?

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So everything depends on who you find?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Exactly. You may find someone excellent who truly supports a child in line with current knowledge." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="But you may also find someone who only calls what they do ABA while actually pushing for politeness, silence, no stimming, and “look me in the eye”." />

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And are there any organizations trying to keep some standards?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>Yes, although this is still more a matter of good practice than hard regulation:</p>
	<ul>
		<li><strong>PSTB – Polish Association for Behavioral Therapy</strong>. They run their own PLTB certification (Polish License for a Behavioral Therapist), organize conferences, and keep a therapist register.</li>
		<li><strong>Instytut Analizy Zachowania</strong> – works with international partners, organizes supervision, and runs its own certification path: Certyfikowany Analityk Zachowania (CAZ).</li>
		<li><strong>Fundacja SCOLAR</strong> – based in Warsaw, offers many trainings and promotes an ethical approach.</li>
	</ul>
</DialogLine>

### What that means in practice

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="But there's no nationwide system like BACB?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="No. In Poland, ABA is still something of a free-for-all. That is why it matters so much to know what to ask — and not take everything on faith just because “behavior analysis” appears in the name." />

## Conclusion: ABA as a tool

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So ABA is not automatically a disaster — but you do need to know who you are working with?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Exactly. ABA is neither a religion nor an enemy — it is a tool." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="And with a tool, you can build a ramp — or a cage. The difference lies in who is holding it." />
