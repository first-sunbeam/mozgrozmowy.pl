### Internal process: what happens when a question is asked?

<DialogLine speaker="me">
	<p>Hey, Brain… can we talk?</p>
	<p>Because when a therapist asks questions like:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>“How are you today?”</li>
		<li>“What brought you here?”</li>
		<li>“What would you like to talk about today?”</li>
		<li>“What do you feel in your body?”</li>
		<li>“What tells you that this is what’s happening?”</li>
		<li>“Can you allow someone to see this?”</li>
	</ul>
	<p>and many others...</p>
	<p>...I often answer: “I don’t know”, “Maybe”, “Nothing specific, really”.</p>
	<p>And you, Brain:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>activate the safety system,</li>
		<li>analyze tone, facial expression, and milliseconds of silence,</li>
		<li>scan all previous experiences,</li>
	</ul>
	<p>and before I say anything, you’ve already calculated everything.</p>
</DialogLine>

## Scene 1: soft question, hard landing

### Question without a vector

<DialogLine speaker="therapist" text="‘What makes you see it this way?’" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>(internal monologue): Wait, what... what exactly do I “see”? This moment? That situation from five years ago? My reaction now? Or something more general?</p>
	<p>This is probably a causality model — but which one? “What makes”, so I’m supposed to identify a cause. But in what model of causality? Psychological, biographical, social?</p>
	<p>This question has no vector. It’s unclear whether it’s about a feeling, data, a belief, or a broader narrative.</p>
	<p>And then there’s that “see”... Does that mean what I think, what I know, what I feel, what I imagine? I perceive things differently from other people. Specifically, literally, in structures.</p>
	<p>And I’m supposed to answer spontaneously even though I don’t know what the question is really asking. I can feel that it’s meant to sound warm and inviting, but my system has no way to process it.</p>
</DialogLine>

### Answer and interpretation

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="‘I don’t know. It just feels that way.’" />
<DialogLine speaker="therapist" text="‘That’s important. Thank you for sharing that with me.’" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="(internal monologue): I don't know what I shared. But it passed. System: frozen, interpretation: uncertain, internal alignment: low." />

### Comment

For a neurotypical person, this question may sound like a neutral, warm invitation to reflect. For an autistic person, it can feel like a message without clear parameters — something that has to be broken down independently before any answer is even possible.

And that is exactly why it is worth adapting language to how the brain works. Not to simplify. To enable access.

## Scene 2: a question with structure

### Question with structure

<DialogLine speaker="therapist" text="‘I’d like to better understand how you came to that conclusion. Can you tell me what specifically led you there?’" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain">
	<p>(internal monologue): Phew. Good. This question has structure. I know what it refers to (“that conclusion”), I know what you want from me (causes), and I know I can reach for data from the past.</p>
	<p>I can move through the folders called “experiences”, “other people’s reactions”, and “situation analysis”. I don’t have to guess tone or intention. There is room for a pause, and there is no empathy test.</p>
</DialogLine>

### Finding meaning

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="‘I think it’s because I’ve encountered that kind of reaction a few times. Since then, I pick up on it faster, before someone even says anything.’" />
<DialogLine speaker="therapist" text="‘That’s very important information. Thank you for showing me that.’" />

### Internal alignment: high!

<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="(internal monologue): Sense found. Answer was possible. System didn't need to defend itself. Internal alignment: high." />

---

## Summary

Adapting language to autistic thinking is not simplification — it is **enabling access**. When a question has structure, direction, and a clear purpose, the brain can respond without activating a defensive system. This is not about ability — it is about communication compatibility.
