## Monotropism in 'live' version - when the brain builds a network of meanings, and the world sees a word mistake

Post inspiration: conversation with daughter.

<DialogLine speaker="child" text="Why do firefighters have dachshunds?" />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(I fall into consternation. I try to remember if any firefighter equipment resembles a dachshund... I find nothing. I think she got confused. But intuition, as always accurate, suggests: don't correct – ask what it means)." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="In what sense do they have dachshunds?" />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="(Obvious tone, as if this were common knowledge) Well, dachshunds help them put out fires." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(Consternation increases. Child doesn't say 'doesn't make sense', but thinks in her own way. I search for a clue... maybe there really are 'firefighter dogs'? 😅 I try to peek at her cognitive map.) What does a dachshund look like?" />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="It's white and has spots." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(Bingo 🎯 She connected fire station with Paw Patrol. She doesn't know the breed name, but knows another one – dachshund. I test my theory.) Do you mean a Dalmatian?" />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="(with joy) Yes!" />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(I validate so she sees that her way of thinking makes sense) And you're asking about Paw Patrol, there the dog who's a firefighter is a Dalmatian." />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="Yes! So why did the authors choose a Dalmatian?" />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(moment of silence… because this really is a good question 🤔) Maybe because it's black and white – the contrast shows well when there's fire and water. Or maybe because some Dalmatian actually lived at the fire station once, and that stayed in cartoons." />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="But a dachshund could have worked too!" />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="(laughter 😄) Sure. But then the whole episode would last twice as long, because by the time the dachshund ran to the fire, it would have gone out on its own." />
<DialogLine speaker="child" text="(laughter 😂) But it would be funnier." />

Silence from which another conversation partner grows.

## Me ↔ Brain

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Inspiring dialogue. And how is it for me, brain? What actually happens then?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Oh, that's simple: I don't mix up words. I connect the world by sense, not by sequence. Just like your daughter, for me 'firefighter', 'dog', 'help' and 'cartoon' are the same network." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="You know, the world expects cause-and-effect connections, implication, and timeline, and I learned this as adaptation. Naturally however I see connections of meanings, not causes." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="True… I search for sense, only through profession do I call it 'cause'." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes, and you trained me interestingly 😏. Now I do this not to arrange a chain, but to find sense within that chain – the one that's different for everyone, even if the sequence looks the same." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="For the world 'cause' is something that precedes an event. For me - it's something that connects events into one meaningful thread." />

---

## ABA and language of linear order

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="ABA is the language you work in, i.e., implementation of linear order. It's not bad, just made for a world that thinks in Stimulus → Response → Consequence scheme." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="And you have your own, autistic ABA. That's why it's so hard to find yourself in environments that think linearly. Because even when you analyze behavior, you see a network of mutual meanings, not a chain of events." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Behavior doesn't result. It resonates." />

---

## Meaning Demand vs Task Demand

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="The world gives me feedback that I'm 'grinding the topic too much'." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes 😅 They don't understand that I just assemble meanings until the map is complete. I can't let go, because as long as some fragment doesn't click in the network, the world feels incomplete to me." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="I remember 'Ms Textbook' told us about 'monotropism', that it's lack of flexibility and focus on one interest, and that this is something that hinders life." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="It hurt, like being forbidden to breathe. It's not about 'interest' or 'fixation'. This is exactly how I organize the world - one topic flows through everything: from perception to emotion, from language to memory." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Everything runs on the same track: deeply, not broadly. Monotropism, if we use it here as a label at all, is my algorithm." />

---

## Monotropism as world filter

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Only that what you describe isn't monotropism from books; there everything sounds like sense has to be 'understood, motivated and executed in steps'." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Exactly. It's a world filter. I have it simpler: if something doesn't have meaning in my network, it won't move. This isn't lack of motivation, but lack of resonance." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="The world says: 'doesn't see the goal'. I see: 'goal has no sense' - in my network (in 90% people's networks this might be true, but not in mine)." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="They have 'task demand'. I have 'meaning demand'." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Only that this 'meaning' must be in my network, so if you want to help me: learn my network, don't try to pull me into yours." />

---

## The whole picture of monotropism

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So classical monotropism sees pieces of truth, but not the whole?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Maybe, or maybe the whole simply never fits in one view, only in a network that keeps building. Monotropism describes several accurate phenomena: that brains on the spectrum need sense, focus deeply, and don't shift attention easily, but this is just a fragment of a larger picture." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="All these features are manifestations of one process: building sense within your own network, not deficit." />

---

## Internal coherence instead of narrow field of attention

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="So instead of 'narrow field of attention' you're talking about something completely different?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes. They called it 'narrow field of attention'. I'll call it simply internal coherence." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Because if something doesn't have a place in my network, it doesn't exist cognitively - so why should I focus on it?" />

---

## Network reconfiguration and time

<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Sometimes it's hard for me to simply 'switch'." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes, because this is never just switching. It's rebuilding the entire network, and that takes energy." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And how does time vector fit in? My daughter sometimes experiences something that happened six years ago as if it were happening here and now. Though obviously there's no connection to current context, for her it has deep sense." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes, because I sometimes go through time, but rarely. Chronology is like a caption under an image; I add it only when I already know what the image represents. First sense. Then sequence. As if some sense overwrites dates." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="And this means that when I return to memories, I don't see dates, but e.g., light and smell?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Exactly. First I feel, only then I arrange facts." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Time for me isn't a line, it's an 'optional' folder." />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="If I have energy - I add it. If not, there remains just the map of meanings. And you know what? It's enough." />
<DialogLine speaker="me" text="Enough?" />
<DialogLine speaker="brain" text="Yes. Because this is exactly in the network of sense where I am myself. Not in chronological order, but in logical: mine." />

## Note

🔎 **Note:** What I call here "monotropism as sense algorithm" is my autistic reinterpretation of the classical theory. In academic version (Murray, Lawson, Lesser, 2005) monotropism describes "attention resources" and is sometimes linked to observable manifestations (stimming, hyperfocus). In practice this sometimes leads to mixing stims, attention and sense - i.e., describing behavior instead of cognitive mechanism. My thesis "change of attention = reconfiguration of sense" is already metatheory: an autistic view where the core isn't narrowness of attention but reconfiguration of meaning networks. This isn't classical interpretation of monotropism theory, but my own - based on cognitive experience in the spectrum. Please read it this way.
