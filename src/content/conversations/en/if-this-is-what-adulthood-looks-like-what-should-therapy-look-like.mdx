<DialogLine
	speaker="me"
	text="Second motif: 'tug of war within' - I want to, but when a demand appears, a block appears"
/>
<DialogLine
	speaker="brain"
	text="This means it's not about lack of motivation. The conflict doesn't run between 'I want' and 'I don't want', but between intention and reaction to pressure. The presenters repeatedly emphasized that the very form of demand can be an activating factor. In this understanding, the block is not a lack of motivation, but a defensive reaction to perceived pressure. When something is formulated as an imposed direction or pace, the nervous system may read it as a loss of autonomy. The block is not then a decision, but a defensive response of the autonomic nervous system. If the therapist's reaction is to increase structure or pace, tension rises."
/>

<DialogLine
	speaker="me"
	text="Relationships were described as exhausting. Not because they aren't important. Because they cost regulatory energy. At one point there was even the ironic 'Hell is other people'."
/>
<DialogLine
	speaker="brain"
	text="This is not rejection of relationships. This is a description of their cost. If the presence of another person requires constant self-monitoring and impression management, the relationship ceases to be neutral. It becomes a burden for the nervous system. In such a situation, even a therapeutic relationship can carry pressure, especially if it contains an element of evaluation, a goal to achieve, or hierarchy. Not coincidentally, the presenters emphasized the importance of authenticity and reduction of hierarchy in the therapeutic relationship. If relationships are regularly costly, the condition of safety becomes the possibility of being in them without the necessity of 'performing' and without tension related to meeting expectations."
/>

<DialogLine
	speaker="me"

<DialogLine
	speaker="brain"
	text="The presenters pointed to frequent patterns of perfectionism and burnout and emphasized the importance of flexibility, co-creation of goals, and limiting imposed structure in therapy for adults with PDA profile. In light of this description, one can hypothesize that if someone maintains efficiency for years at the cost of over-mobilization, perfectionism serves as control function, and guilt becomes an internal drive to further exceed boundaries. Burnout would not then be a lack of strength, but the cost of such a mode of operation. In such a context, increasing tolerance for demands may perpetuate overload."
