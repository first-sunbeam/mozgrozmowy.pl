_(The PDA conference in Perth on November 11, 2025)_

Kate Heina is an occupational therapist who has worked with neurodivergent people for 16 years. She's also the mother of a child with a PDA profile. In her talk, she explains the difference between **capability and capacity**, and how we should match what we expect from a child with what they can actually handle at the moment.

## Let's start.

The speech is based on Ross Greene's idea: Kids do well if they can. When we say "difficulty," we don't mean _willful defiance_; we mean that the demands being made are more than the child can handle at the moment. Behavior is a way for kids to show if they're handling pressure, both the kind they mean to put on themselves and the kind they don't.

## The nervous system and safety

Kate bases this reasoning on neurobiology, referencing Stephen Porges' polyvagal theory and the concept of neuroception, which is basically the automatic, non-conscious monitoring of the environment for safety.

It's important to note that the autonomic nervous system functions independently of conscious control. In this case, it's not a decision but a physiological state.

When the system **detects safety**, meaning when it's in a state of safety, you can access executive functions like learning, planning, communication, and relationship building. When **threat is detected**, the stress response kicks in and access to the "thinking part of the brain" becomes limited.

For people with a PDA profile, the threat-detection system might be especially sensitive. Stressors like demands, deadlines, loss of control, and violations of autonomy might all show up as threat cues.

## Cumulative stress and the “bucket”

Kate mentions the idea of cumulative stress, which Stuart Shanker talks about in his work. Stress keeps building up. The metaphor of **the bucket** shows the total available energy, or the capacity to deal with everyday demands.

Demands and pressure act like a running tap. Every extra thing you add to your plate adds more weight to the bucket. Stressors build up and slowly narrow the **window of tolerance** — a term popularized by Dan Siegel to describe the range of optimal arousal.

As long as the load stays within the acceptable range, the child can use their capabilities. When the bucket overflows, it's like a **fight-or-flight** situation. You can see it when people **freeze** up or **shutdown**.

## Capability versus capacity

The word **capability** simply means that a certain skill is present.

**Capacity** is basically how much regulatory resources there are to use a skill at a given moment. One day someone might be able to do a lot, and the next day they might not be able to do as much. This could be because they're tired, sick, stressed, or dealing with something from the past.

Skills don't just disappear. They might be temporarily inaccessible. In that sense, this is about **can't, not won't** Your capacity is determined by your current state of the nervous system, not by your willpower.

## Aligning expectations

If capacity is variable, expectations must also be flexible. Kate uses the phrase **aligning expectations**, which means adjusting expectations to match what's currently possible.

This is a shift from putting more pressure on them to adapting demands to the current regulatory state. Basically, it's about understanding how different tasks cost the child, and when it's good to push them to try something harder (**reach up**) and when it's better to ease off (**drop**).

## Three levels of capacity

Kate suggests three approximate levels of capacity:

### 1. Very low capacity / Recovery

There's not a lot of capacity. Functioning focuses on basic self-care and the safest possible environment. People's expectations are pretty low. Recovery takes time, so you can't rush it.

### 2. Stability

Moderate capacity. It becomes possible to gradually expand the range of safe people, places, and activities.

### 3. Stretch days

There are periods when increased capacity becomes possible, and more demanding activities can be undertaken. These require limiting other burdens in advance and substantial recovery afterward.

These levels can go up or down.

## Monitoring the here and now

In addition to capacity, it's important to keep checking if the child's still within their tolerance range. So, the big question is: **Do you think we're close to hitting the tipping point of overwhelm**?

Here are some early signs that your capacity might be dropping:

- procrastination,
- retreat into fantasy,
- more sensory sensitivity
- greater need for control
- changes in communication
- more movement and physical tension.

At the moment, you're deciding whether to support **reach up** or apply **drop**. It's not a rigid procedure or algorithm, but a process that requires attention and attunement.

## Ending

Finding the right balance between capability and capacity is tricky. Mistakes happen. The basic idea is that trust is key. Kids will do what they can when they're ready, not when they feel pressured.

## PS. Who does Kate Heina refer to?

### Ross W. Greene

A clinical psychologist and the creator of the Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS) model. He is the author of the phrase "Kids do well if they can," which posits that challenging behaviors stem from missing skills or overload, not from bad intent.

### Dr. Stephen Porges

A neurophysiologist and the originator of the polyvagal theory. He describes how the autonomic nervous system responds to cues of safety and threat, and how these responses shape access to cognitive and relational functions.

### Dan Siegel

A psychiatrist and a researcher in the field of interpersonal neurobiology. He popularized the concept of the "window of tolerance," which describes the range of arousal in which optimal cognitive and emotional functioning is possible.

### Stuart Shanker

A developmental psychologist whose research is focused on self-regulation. He characterizes stress as cumulative, emphasizing that behavior serves as an indicator of the load carried by the nervous system.
